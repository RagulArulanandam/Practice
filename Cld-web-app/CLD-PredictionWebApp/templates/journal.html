Cassava Leaf Disease Identification Using Deep Learning Techniques.

Abstract

Our work is to develop a model through deep learning techniques to identify the disease which affect the Cassava Plant. A set 21,000 sample images were collected and categorized into four classes of diseases are Cassava Mosaic Disease (CMD), Cassava Bacterial Blight (CBB), Cassava Green Mite (CGM), Cassava Brown Streak (CBS). These sample images were taken on the agricultural field in Uganda and annotated by experts at the NCRRI with the AI lab at University in Kampala. This is in a format that most realistically represents what farmers would need to diagnose in real life. An accurate model was created using Convolution Neural Network (CNN) to identify the affected/unaffected leaves. And this model was converted into a web application for a better identification. This model had an 70%-85% accuracy.

Cassava is a starchy root vegetable or tuber with a nutty taste. It's a big source of calories and carbohydrates for people in developing countries, and it's native to South America. Its biological name is Manihot esculenta and the common names are macaxeira, mandioca, aipim, agbei, manioc and yuca. Cassava is a calorie-dense vegetable that is high in carbohydrates as well as essential vitamins and minerals. Vitamin C, thiamine, riboflavin, and niacin are all found in abundance in cassava. The leaves, which can also be cooked or dried in the sun, can contain up to 25% protein. Cassava is a perennial plant with prominent, almost palmate (fan-shaped) leaves that are similar to those of the related castor oil plant but have five to nine lobes. The fleshy roots have a similar texture to dahia tubers. Low herbs to branching shrubs and slender unbranched trees are among the several types. Others have adapted to dry alkaline soil zones, while others have adapted to acid mud banks along rivers.



Cassava is one of the most drought-tolerant crops, can be grown successfully on poor soils, and produces fair yields in areas where many other crops fail. Cassava grows well at latitudes 30° north and south of the equator, at elevations between sea level and 2,000 m (7,000 ft) above sea level, in equatorial temperatures, with annual rainfalls ranging from 50 to 5,000 mm (2 to 200 in), and at elevations between sea level and 2,000 m (7,000 ft) above sea level. Bad soils ranging from acidic to alkaline in pH. In some parts of Africa and South America, these conditions are normal. Farmers across Africa are growing cassava on a small, medium, and large scale in a variety of environmental and climatic conditions, contributing to food security and industrial crops, but the main challenge is that cassava plants are susceptible to a wide range of pathogens, including less well-known viral strains. It is an annual root crop that grows in tropical and subtropical regions and the most widely grown root crop that produces an edible tuber which is a third major source of carbohydrates. 2.Literature review

This starchy root is grown in Africa, but viral diseases are a major cause of low yields. Cassava green mite (CGM), cassava bacterial blight (CBB), cassava mosaic disease (CMD), and cassava brown streak disease are some of the diseases that affect cassava (CBSD).



1. CGM (cassava green mite)

White spots occur on the leaves as a result of it. It begins as small spots that expand to cover the entire leaf surface, causing chlorophyll loss and thus affecting photosynthesis. In addition to mottling symptoms that are easily mistaken with cassava mosaic, extreme CGM causes the infected leaves to dry out, shrink, and break away from the plant.



2. CBB (Cassava Bacterial Blight)

Moisture is to blame for this bacterial infection. Cassava plants in wet areas are thus the most vulnerable. Black leaf spots and blights are the most common symptoms. As a result of wilting, the infected leaves dry prematurely and shed.

3.CBSD (Casava Brown Streak Disease)

Symptoms include a distinctive vein yellow that sometimes enlarges and forms visually broad yellow patching. CBSD also causes dark-brown necrotic areas on the tuber base, as well as a decrease in root size. These variations are due to differences in growing conditions, plant age, and virus types. Brown streaks can appear on the cassava plant's stems. In addition, there is a dry brown-black necrotic rot of the cassava tuber that may spread from a small lesion to the entire root.

4.CMD (Cassava Mosaic Disease)

Cassava mosaic virus is the common name for any of eleven distinct plant pathogenic viruses belonging to the genus Begom virus. It causes mottling, mosaic rust, twisted leaves, and a general reduction in the size of leaves, as well as the affected plants. Patches of green are often mixed in with the various colours of yellow and white patches on leaves. The patches minimise photosynthesis surface area, resulting in stunted growth and low yield.

5.1 Model Development

The development of deep learning model trained process is shown.











Deep learning is the part of the machine learning which is used to handle the datasets which comes under Artificial Intelligence, it categorized in two types supervised and unsupervised. To carry out the machine learning process, it employs a hierarchical level of artificial neural networks. Artificial neural networks are designed in the same way as the human brain, with neuron nodes linked in a web-like pattern. Although conventional programmes construct analysis with data in a linear manner, deep learning systems' hierarchical function allows machines to process data in a nonlinear manner. This model had an input/output layer sandwiched between a slew of hidden layers that were all linked together.

TensorFlow was built with massive numerical computations in mind, not with deep learning in mind.



















CNN (Convolutional Neural Network): Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) are one of the most well-known deep learning algorithms, and they're also one of the most widely used in image classification. Convolutional layers, pooling layers, and completely linked layers are the three types of layers used in the CNN architecture. The CNN algorithm takes an input image and processes it across layers to classify features and recognize the image before generating a classification result. Convolutional and pooling layers alternate in the architecture of the CNN, which is accompanied by a series of completely linked layers. Each layer's output becomes the input for the next layer in the CNN.

A) Image Input Layer: The image size is defined in an imageInputLayer, which in this case is 28-by-28-by-1. The height, width, and channel size are all represented by these numbers. Since the digit data is in grayscale, the channel size (color channel) is 1. The channel size for a color picture is 3, which corresponds to the RGB values. You don't need to shuffle the data because train Network does so automatically at the start of the training. During preparation, train Network can also automatically shuffle the data at the start of each epoch.



B) Convolutional Layer: The first claim in the convolutional layer is filter Size, which is the height and width of the filters used by the training function when scanning the images. The number 3 means that the filter size is 3-by-3 in this case. The height and width of the filter can be specified in various sizes. The second argument, Num Filters, is the number of filters, which is the number of neurons that bind to the same input field. The number of feature maps is determined by this parameter. To add padding to the input function map, use the 'Padding' name-value pair. 'Same' padding guarantees that the spatial output size is the same as the input size for a convolutional layer with a default stride of 1. The stride and learning rates for this layer can also be described using the name-value pair arguments of convolution2dLayer.



C)Batch Normalization Layer: The activations and gradients propagating through a network are normalised by batch normalisation layers, making network training a simpler optimization problem. To speed up network training and minimise sensitivity to network initialization, use batch normalisation layers between convolutional layers and nonlinearities, such as ReLU layers. To make a batch normalisation layer, use batchNormalizationLayer.



D) ReLU Layer: A nonlinear activation function follows the batch normalisation layer. The rectified linear unit is the most common activation function (ReLU). To render a ReLU sheet, use reluLayer.



E)FullyConnectedLayer: One or more fully connected layers follow the convolutional and downsampling layers. A completely connected layer, as the name implies, is one in which all of the neurons in the preceding layer are connected. To recognise the larger patterns, this layer combines all of the features learned from the previous layers across theiimage.The final completely linked layer incorporates the features for image classification. As a consequence, the number of classes in the target data equals the OutputSize parameter in the last fully connected layer. The output size in this example is ten, which corresponds to the ten classes. To make a completely connected layer, use fullyConnectedLayer.



F) SoftMax Layer: The fully connected layer's output is normalised using the SoftMax activation function. The SoftMax layer produces a set of positive numbers that add up to one, which the classification layer can use as classification probabilities. After the last completely connected layer, use the SoftMax Layer feature to build a SoftMax layer.



G) Classification Layer: The classification layer is the last layer. This layer assigns each input to one of the mutually exclusive groups and computes the loss using the probabilities returned by the SoftMax activation function. Using classification Layer to render a classification layer.

5.4 Model Architecture

Using the dataset, train test split occurs to train the model. An implementation of keras was used in that Image Data Generator to supplement the images in real-time while your model was still practising! As each training image is passed to the model, you can add any random transformations you want. This will not only make your model more stable, but it will also save you memory. It was very helpful to reshape and rescale the images for train and validation process. EfficientNetB3 and GlobalAveragePooling2D were used to reshaping the image for creating a model with many hidden layers. EfficientNet is a convolutional neural network architecture and scaling approach that uses a compound coefficient to scale all depth/width/resolution dimensions uniformly. GlobalAveragePooling2D average pools the spatial dimensions until each is one, then leaves the other dimensions alone. Fitting the model with epoch of 5 and step size of validation and training set with batch size is 32.Epoch is once all images are processed one time individually of forward and backward to the network. And the final model is saved as a Cassava_modelEffNetB3.h5 file.





















For better performance and accuracy retrain the model again with epoch of 3 and batch size of 32 shown below. Then save and fit the model as Cassava_modelEffNetB3V3.h5 file. This is the final model which is used to predict the disease and shown in the web application.





During training, there were two types of parameters: those that were learned from the model, such as the weights, and those that were tuned, such as learning speeds, the number of epochs, batch size, input form, optimizer, and the number of neurons in the hidden layers, which were all hyper-parameters. The impact of different input image dimensions on the accuracy of CNN models was addressed. So, as CNN was practising, it used some of these hyper-parameters; however, the model was improved by using tuning techniques to find some of the best values for these hyper-parameters. The explanation for this is that neural networks are difficult to configure and have a lot of parameters to set up, not to mention that individual models can take a long time to train.
